Life Beyond Code

My Hobbies

I believe who you are outside the terminal defines the depth you bring to the work inside it. Here are the two things I keep returning to — no matter how busy the semester gets.

01

Reading

Books that have shaped how I think

Reading is my slowest and most rewarding habit. I gravitate toward non-fiction — especially philosophy, productivity, and technology — but I'll pick up a well-recommended novel without hesitation. There's something powerful about sitting with a book that was written decades ago and realizing it's still describing today.

Marcus Aurelius's Meditations is the book I return to most. It's not a story — it's a collection of private reminders a Roman emperor wrote to himself. That rawness makes it more honest than most modern self-help ever manages to be.

I also enjoy reading about software craft — books like Clean Code and The Pragmatic Programmer read less like textbooks and more like mentorship from engineers who've already made every mistake you're about to make.

02

Travelling

Places I've been and places I'm headed

Travelling changes your reference frame. It's the most effective way I know to realize that most of what feels like "normal" is actually just local — a product of where you grew up, not how things have to be. Every trip recalibrates something.

Growing up in Dhaka — one of the world's most densely packed cities — I've always been drawn to places that feel like the opposite: mountains, open coastlines, quiet towns. Bangladesh itself has more of this than most people realize.

My favourite kind of travel is slow travel — staying long enough in a place that you stop being a tourist and start noticing how people actually live. I'm less interested in checking off landmarks and more interested in finding a good local café and watching the city from inside it.
